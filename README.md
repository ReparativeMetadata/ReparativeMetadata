# Reparative Metadata
## A Brief Introduction
Welcome to the reparative metadata page, part of the Center for Information Discovery, Experimentation, and Access Strategies (IDEAS)!
This page is all about finding, strengthening, and experimenting with new ways to connect information seekers with information itself. A key part of this work is recognizing that you can tweak algorithms, searches, AI prompts, and any number of other factors all you want - if the original source isn't appropriate identified, you'll never find it.

Humans love organizing. In language, in spaces (physical or digital), and in our lives broadly, organizing helps us keep track of things and get things done. A desk that is organized, for example, allows you to quickly and easily find the thing you're looking for (a pen, paper, a USB cord). When we get used to patterns of organization, it helps us navigate unfamiliar spaces. Think, for example, about how grocery stores tend to be organized similarly so you know approximately where to go in one even if you've never been to that location before. Imagine how difficult it would be to find eggs if they were located in different places in every store!

Patterns of organization also happen at the level of language and of data. When a movie is identified as "Horror," a song as "Pop," or a video game as a "Soulslike," that linguistically 'tags' that item. The tag places the item in a category with others like it, and doing so allows things like algorithms, search engines, AI, and websites to show you more of "similar" items. It helps you discover new things that (in theory) you're more likely to enjoy or find useful than if, say, your next recommendation were <i>completely</i> random.

Sometimes this works well, or works well enough. But in other cases existing tags don't meet certain kinds of needs. For example, if you like horror but too much blood and gore makes you sick, then an algorithm recommending you watch <i>A Nightmare on Elm Street</i> or <i>Friday the 13<sup>th</sup></i> is not making a good recommendation - but the "Horror" tag alone will not tell you that. What you need is additional information; what you need is additional metadata. If movies were also tagged with information like "Low blood/gore," "Medium blood/gore," etc., then you would be more likely to find horror films that you might actually enjoy.

Reparative metadata is all about creating metadata that better supports the range of information needs and wants of learners and researchers. 

## Metadata History
Historically, metadata was mostly created by librarians and adhered to very strict standards around what kinds of language could (or could not) be used. Today, while libraries are striving to update their lanugage and practices, they are often hindered by layers of bureaucracy and historical sampling biases (many metadata patterns were established in the early 20th century based on copyright holdings of the Library of Congress in the U.S., for example).
